Circuit Engineering

Website UX Audit

A self-initiated website audit and competitor benchmark for Circuit Engineering, a PCB manufacturing and electronics services company based in Elk Grove Village, Illinois.

View Audit Report See Findings

Role

UX audit, competitive review, report writing

Project Type

Self-initiated website evaluation

Context

Experience-building audit for a live company website

Timeline

December 2025

Context

I used a real company website to practice practical UX evaluation beyond class assignments.

I completed this audit independently to gain experience evaluating a live company website in a technical industry. The goal was not to redesign the site from scratch, but to identify practical areas where clarity, trust, navigation, and quote readiness could improve.

After I shared the audit, the team responded positively and discussed the possibility of a future project-based opportunity through an approved academic internship path. I treated the audit as a learning experience and a way to understand how UX evaluation can support real business communication.

Audit Scope

The review focused on how well the site builds confidence for technical buyers.

01

Visual design and branding

Reviewing hierarchy, brand consistency, typography, imagery, and how professional the site feels at first glance.

02

Navigation and structure

Checking whether users can find services, capabilities, contact information, and quote paths without friction.

03

Content clarity

Looking at whether the site explains what the company does, who it serves, and why a buyer should trust it.

04

Trust signals

Checking for certifications, proof elements, facility cues, compliance information, and credibility markers.

05

Performance and accessibility

Reviewing mobile responsiveness, page speed concerns, heading structure, alt text, focus states, and form feedback.

06

Competitor benchmarks

Comparing Circuit Engineering against six PCB and EMS competitors with stronger digital presentation patterns.

Benchmarking

The competitor review helped separate personal opinion from visible industry patterns.

I compared Circuit Engineering with American Standard Circuits, Eagle Electronics, Green Circuits, Advanced Circuits, Bay Area Circuits, and Royal Circuits.

The goal was to see what strong PCB and EMS websites were doing better: clearer quote paths, stronger hero sections, richer visual media, visible certifications, and more detailed service information.

Circuit Engineering

Functional but understated

The site was direct and easy to scan, but lacked strong messaging, service depth, and visible trust cues.

Competitors

Clearer conversion paths

Peer sites made quote requests, certifications, facility proof, and service claims more visible.

Opportunity

Improve without a rebuild

The recommendations focused on practical upgrades instead of proposing an unrealistic full redesign.

Key Findings

The site had a clear foundation, but it needed stronger guidance and credibility.

01

Quote path was not obvious enough

The upload portal existed, but it was not clearly framed as the main quote path. This made the primary conversion action harder to notice.

02

Value proposition felt too vague

The site explained services, but did not strongly state who the ideal customer was or why buyers should choose this firm.

03

Services and capabilities overlapped

Similar content appeared across sections, which made it harder for engineers and buyers to know where specifications lived.

04

Trust cues were limited

Certifications, proof points, team visibility, facility images, and compliance information were not prominent enough.

05

Hero areas needed clearer direction

The top sections were present, but they lacked strong subheadlines and calls to action that could guide the first click.

06

Technical hygiene had practical gaps

The review noted concerns around image load, heading structure, alt text coverage, focus states, form states, and analytics visibility.

Recommendations

I organized the recommendations around changes that could improve trust and conversion without rebuilding everything.

01

Make “Request a quote” visible sitewide

Add quote CTAs in the top navigation and hero areas, rename the upload portal, and connect the flow to a short pre-upload form.

02

Clarify the homepage message

Use a direct headline and subheadline that explain the company’s PCB services, local support, quality, and turnaround value.

03

Separate services from technical specifications

Use Services as a hub, then support deeper sections with materials, layer counts, tolerances, finishes, lead times, and use cases.

04

Surface proof earlier

Add certification blocks, facility photos, client proof, project counts, and a short confidentiality message near quote actions.

05

Improve structure and accessibility

Clean up heading hierarchy, add alt text, strengthen focus states, show form success/error states, and test key pages at mobile widths.

What I Learned

This was an early audit, but it helped me understand how UX work connects to business trust.

Real websites require practical judgment

The most useful recommendations were not the most dramatic ones. They were the changes that could realistically improve clarity, trust, and contact readiness without disrupting the whole site.

Industry context matters

For a PCB and EMS company, credibility is not only visual polish. Buyers also look for specifications, certifications, compliance cues, quote clarity, and proof of technical capability.

I would make it stronger now

Since completing this audit, I have learned more about usability testing, structured reporting, and evidence-based evaluation. If I revisited it, I would add more user tasks, clearer severity ratings, and a more formal testing method.

Reflection

This audit gave me a practical bridge between UX evaluation and real business communication.

Circuit Engineering’s website already had a simple structure and direct communication style. The opportunity was to make the site feel more credible, more guided, and more useful for technical buyers comparing multiple vendors.

The project helped me practice turning observations into recommendations that a business could realistically act on. It also showed me how much trust depends on small details: quote paths, proof elements, terminology, structure, and the confidence of the first screen.
